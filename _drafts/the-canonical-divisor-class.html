When, as a physicist, I want to read papers dealing with F-theory, I often encounter concepts from algebraic geometry with which I am not at ease. One typical example is the object
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{O}(- n K_B) \, ,
\end{equation}
where $n$ is a positive integer and $B$ is a complex surface. This appears for instance in this article (see equation 2.1) when describing an elliptically fibered Calabi-Yau over the base $B$. To be more precise, the authors consider a singular fibration $\pi : X \rightarrow B$ with an equation
\begin{equation}
y^2 = x^3 + fx + g \, ,
\end{equation}
where $f$ and $g$ are sections of the line bundles $\mathcal{O}(- 4 K_B) $ and $\mathcal{O}(- 6 K_B) $. In this post, I would like to pause and explain what this means, using only as a prerequisite what a (theoretical !) physicist hopefully knows. Obviously, the goal is to understand the important ideas, and I will not try to make a very rigorous nor a very general presentation. I will dedicate another post to the physical content of this construction.

Divisors in algebraic geometry

A divisor is a generalization of the concept of a codimension 1 subvariety in an algebraic variety, that applies to a larger class of objects (schemes, etc). Let us focus on algebraic varieties. A (Weil) divisor is a formal finite sum of irreducible closed subvarieties of codimension 1, weighted by integer coefficients,
\begin{equation}
\sum\limits_i n_i Z_i \, .
\end{equation}
For instance, if the variety has dimension 1 (think about a Riemann surface), then a (Weil) divisor is a weighted sum of points (the points are the codimension 1 subvarieties !).

Let us stay on a Riemann surface for a while. A meromorphic function $f$ defined on this surface has a finite number of isolated zeros and poles, and it is natural to define the divisor of $f$ as
\begin{equation}
(f) := \sum\limits_z \mathrm{ord}_z (f) z \, .
\end{equation}
In this formula, the letter $z$ denotes the points of the Riemann surface. Any divisor that can be written $(f)$ for some meromorphic function $f$ is called a principal divisor, and two divisors that differ by a principal divisor are called linearly equivalent. As the name suggests, this is an equivalence relation, and the principal divisors belong to the trivial equivalence class, that contains $0$. A non-trivial class is the canonical divisor class, which is the class of the divisors of nonzero meromorphic one-forms (any such one-form can be obtained from any other by multiplication by a meromorphic function, so there is indeed a unique class). If the surface is called $X$, any divisor in the canonical divisor class is called a canonical divisor, and is denoted
\begin{equation}
K_X \, .
\end{equation}

This can be generalized to higher dimensional varieties. Let $X$ be a $d$-dimensional algebraic variety, and $f$ be a non-zero rational function on $X$. We can define
\begin{equation}
(f) := \sum\limits_Z \mathrm{ord}_Z (f) Z \, .
\end{equation}
Now the sum runs over the irreducible closed sets $Z$ of codimension 1. One can then consider the divisor of a rational $d$-form $\omega$. Its class, up to linear equivalence, is called the canonical divisor $K_X$.

Divisors and Line Bundles

Let us consider a divisor $D$ on a variety $X$. One can construct an object $\mathcal{O}_X (D)$ from this divisor (it is a coherent sheaf, but we won't need this), which is made of rational functions $f$ on $X$ such that
\begin{equation}
(f) + D \geq 0 \, .
\end{equation}
More properly, we say that $f$ is a section of $\mathcal{O}_X (D)$. Concretely, if $D$ is the divisor of a function $g$, $\mathcal{O}_X (D)$ is made of the functions $f$ such that $fg$ has no zeros.
