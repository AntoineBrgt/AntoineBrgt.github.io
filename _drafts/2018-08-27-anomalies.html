In QFT, anomalies play a crucial role. There are several kinds of anomalies, and it is not always clear what are the implications of having a non-vanishing anomaly of each kind.

The appearance of anomalies can be seen through various points of views:

  • Let's recall that a classical symmetry is a symmetry of the classical action, a transformation of the fields that leaves the action invariant. In the path integral formalism of QFT, if the measure is invariant under these symmetries, they translate to Ward identities for correlators or Slavnov-Taylor identities for the quantum effective action. When that's not the case, the identities are violated by an anomaly.
  • impossibility of regulating a theory while preserving a classical symmetry (explain... see http://gsfp.physi.uni-heidelberg.de/graddays/content/en/zubehoer/anhaenge/serone/Anomalies_Review_9.10.2016.pdf).
  • Conservation of a classically conserved current at the quantum level.

Implications of an anomaly :

  • For global symmetries : classical selection rules are not obeyed, classically forbidden processes can occur in the quantum theory.
  • For a gauge "symmetry" : an anomaly would spoil unitarity and renormalizability (potentially).

Another kind of anomaly is the Witten anomaly.

Global anomalies

For instance we have rescaling anomaly, dimensional transmutation. The quantum theory is no longer scale invariant.

other example : axial anomaly.

The chiral anomaly. Consider a massless Dirac fermion in $2n$ dimensions in a representation $\mathcal{R}$ of a gauge group $G$. The classical Lagrangian is invariant under $\psi \rightarrow e^{i \alpha \gamma_{2n+1}} \psi$, where $\gamma_{2n+1}$ is the chirality matrix in $2n$ dimensions. The associated classically conserved current is called the chiral current. But because the fermion measure in the path integral is not invariant under the chiral symmetry, we have a quantum anomaly: $$\delta_{\alpha} [\mathcal{D} \psi \mathcal{D} \bar{\psi}] = [\mathcal{D} \psi \mathcal{D} \bar{\psi}] \exp \left( -2i \int \alpha \mathcal{A} \right) \, . $$

Gauge anomalies

No anomaly at all, or cancellation of anomalies is required.

Witten anomalies

"It is also a gauge anomaly" (https://physik.uni-graz.at/~axm/anomalies2013.pdf). This is a problem exclusively applying to the $Sp(N)$ (that includes $SU(2)$ of course) and $O(2< N< 6)$ gauge theories. This depends on the fourth homotopy group of the gauge group.

We have $\pi_4(U(N)) = \pi_4(SU(N))$ for all positive integers $N$ (https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/2454985/homotopy-groups-un-and-sun-pi-mun-pi-msun). We have $\pi_4(U(N)) = 0$ for all positive integers $N$ except for $N=2$, where $\pi_4(U(2)) = \mathbb{Z}_2$.

We have $\pi_4(SO(N)) = 0$ for all positive integers $N$ except for $N=3,4,5$, where $\pi_4(SO(3)) = \pi_4(SO(5)) = \mathbb{Z}_2$ and $\pi_4(SO(4)) = \mathbb{Z}_2^2 $.

We have $\pi_4(Sp(N)) = \mathbb{Z}_2$ for all positive integers $N$.

We have $\pi_4(G) = 0$ for the five exceptional groups $G$.
