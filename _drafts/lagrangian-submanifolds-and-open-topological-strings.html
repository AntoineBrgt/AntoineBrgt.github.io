First of all, what is a Lagrangian submanifold ? This concept arises naturally in classical mechanics, where the system under consideration has a phase space, which is a symplectic manifold, and a real-valued function $H$ defined on this manifold, the Hamiltonian. Combining the symplectic structure and the Hamiltonian, one constructs the Hamiltonian vector field on the phase space, which gives the evolution of the system.

The phase space has even dimension, let us call it $2n$. Then a Lagrangian submanifold is simply a submanifold of dimension $n$ on which the symplectic form vanishes.[1. A submanifold on which the symplectic form vanishes but which does not necessarily have the maximal dimension $n$ is called co-isotropic.] To give an example, the fiber $T^{\ast}_p M$ of the cotangent bundle $T^{\ast} M$ at point $p \in M$ is a Lagrangian submanifold, and very importantly, so is its image by the Hamiltonian flow after any specified time lapse, although this image is no longer a fiber of the form $T^{\ast}_{p'} M$ in general.[1. More can be found about this point of view here. ]

Let us now move to Kähler geometry. Calabi-Yau manifolds are special cases of Kähler manifolds, and quoting Wikipedia,

a Kähler manifold is a manifold with three mutually compatible structures: a complex structure, a Riemannian structure, and a symplectic structure.

In particular, it has a symplectic structure given by a symplectic 2-form $\omega$, and the concept of Lagrangian submanifold makes sense. If we want to discuss a topological sigma model in which the worldsheet is a non-compact Riemann surface (which is characterized by its genus and its number of holes), we have to specify boundary conditions, and the Lagrangian submanifolds of the target Calabi-Yau space are precisely the manifolds on which the boundaries have to be sent in order to preserve topological invariance.
