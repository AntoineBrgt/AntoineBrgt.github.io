First we will define the blow-up of the affine space $\mathbb{A}^n$ at the origin, that I call $O$. For this, we choose coordinates $x_1,\dots , x_n$ on $\mathbb{A}^n$ and $y_1 , \dots , y_n$ on the projective space $\mathbb{P}^{n-1}$. These are homogeneous coordinates, i.e. subjected to the usual equivalence relation. The blow-up of $\mathbb{A}^n$ at $O$ is $$X = \{ (x_1 , \dots , x_n , y_1 , \dots , y_n) \in \mathbb{A}^n \times \mathbb{P}^{n-1} | x_i y_j = x_j y_i \textrm{ for all } i,j=1,\dots,n\} \,. $$ There is a natural map $$\varphi : X \rightarrow \mathbb{A}^n$$ obtained from the projection map $\mathbb{A}^n \times \mathbb{P}^{n-1} \rightarrow \mathbb{A}^n$ by restriction. The set $X$ and the map $\varphi$ have several interesting properties :

  • $\varphi$ gives an isomorphism $X-\varphi^{-1}(O) \rightarrow \mathbb{A}^n-\{O\}$.
  • $\varphi^{-1}(O)$ is in bijection with the lines of $\mathbb{A}^n$ passing through $O$, and therefore is isomporphic to $\mathbb{P}^{n-1}$.
  • $X$ is irreducible.
Now we can come to the really interesting point: the definition of the blow-up of a variety. Let $Y$ be a subvariety of $\mathbb{A}^n$ passing through $O$. The blowing-up of $Y$ at $O$ is $$\widetilde{Y} = \overline{\varphi^{-1}(Y-\{O\})} \,.$$

Let's immediately look at an example. We consider the curve $$Y=\left\{(x,y) \in \mathbb{A}^2 \mid y^2=x^2(x+1) \right\} \,. $$ We call $t$ and $u$ a pair of homogeneous coordinates on $\mathbb{P}^1$, so that the equation of the blow-up $X$ is $xu=ty$. Then for $(x,y,t,u) \in \mathbb{A}^2 \times \mathbb{P}^{1} $, we have \begin{eqnarray*} (x,y,t,u) \in \varphi^{-1}(Y-\{O\}) & \Longleftrightarrow & xu=ty \textrm{ and } y^2=x^2(x+1) \textrm{ and } (x,y) \neq (0,0) \end{eqnarray*} Now we can choose a patch of $\mathbb{P}^{1}$, say the one where $t \neq 0$. There, we can choose $t=1$, and $u \in \mathbb{A}^{1}$. In that patch, $\varphi^{-1}(Y-\{O\})$ is a subset of $\mathbb{A}^{3}$ that is given by the equations \begin{eqnarray*} xu=y \textrm{ and } y^2=x^2(x+1) \textrm{ and } (x,y) \neq (0,0) & \Longleftrightarrow & xu=y \textrm{ and } x^2(x+1-u^2)=0 \textrm{ and } (x,y) \neq (0,0) \\ & \Longleftrightarrow & xu=y \textrm{ and } u^2=x+1 \textrm{ and } (x,y) \neq (0,0) \end{eqnarray*} As a consequence, we obtain the curve \begin{equation*} \begin{cases} u^2=x+1 \\ y=xu \end{cases} \end{equation*}

In the above picture, the initial curve is in orange, the blowup is in blue. In orange is the $\mathbb{P}^{1}$, which we call the exceptional curve $E$. Of course here we see only one patch of $E$, the patch parametrized by $u$ and whose equation is $x=y=0$. One point is missing, namely $t=0$ (or equivalently $u=\infty$). The blowup $\widetilde{Y}$ meets $E$ at two points, $u=\pm 1$, which correspond to the two slopes of the initial curve at $O$.
